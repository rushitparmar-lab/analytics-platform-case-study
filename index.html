The Challenge

A growing B2B company's marketing and sales teams had no shared view of their own performance. Campaign data lived across five disconnected platforms — Google Analytics, HubSpot, LinkedIn, Twitter, and Brightalk webinars — with no automated way to bring it together.

Each week, staff spent hours manually pulling data from each platform, copy-pasting into brittle spreadsheets, and reconciling conflicting numbers. The result was slow, error-prone reporting that nobody fully trusted. Worse, critical knowledge about data sources and definitions was locked in individual employees' heads rather than documented as enterprise practice.

Campaign attribution was impossible. Forecasting was guesswork. When someone left the team, institutional knowledge walked out the door with them.

Marketing and sales had no shared view of pipeline performance — they were making strategic decisions based on numbers they didn't fully trust, from reports that were already out of date.

Core problems identified:

  • —Manual data collection consuming significant hours per week across both teams
  • —Five siloed platforms with no unified data layer or shared definitions
  • —Campaign attribution impossible — no single source of truth across channels
  • —Critical data knowledge held by individuals, not documented as enterprise standards
  • —No formal data ownership, governance, or quality controls
  • —Reporting was backward-looking, slow, and inconsistent between teams

The Approach

The engagement covered architecture design, platform build-out, governance framework, and analytics products — structured across three sequential phases.

Phase 01 Architecture & Integration

Designed and built an end-to-end analytics platform integrating all five marketing data sources into a governed Canonical Data Model. Automated ETL pipelines — built in Python on Azure, with data landed into Snowflake — replaced the weekly manual data collection entirely. The pipeline architecture was designed for reliability and observability, with clear data contracts between source systems and the warehouse layer.

Phase 02 Governance & Data Culture

Established formal data ownership, access controls, and quality standards that had previously existed only as individual habits. The work here was as much organizational as technical — extracting undocumented institutional knowledge from employees and formalizing it into enterprise-wide definitions, ontologies, and data ownership frameworks that the organization could depend on, audit, and scale.

This phase transformed the organization's relationship with its own data: from tribal knowledge locked in silos to a governed, shared asset with clear accountability at every layer.

Data Governance Enterprise Ontology Data Ownership Framework Quality Standards Access Controls
Phase 03 Analytics Products & Predictive Models

Built dashboards that gave marketing and sales a shared, real-time view of campaign performance, pipeline progress, and engagement metrics across all integrated platforms. For the first time, both teams were looking at the same numbers — unbiased, live, and actionable.

On top of the unified data layer, engineered and deployed Python-based regression models for customer churn prediction and customer fit scoring. The models drew on three feature sets: CRM data (contract history, engagement patterns, account tenure), historical revenue and project data, and web traffic signals reflecting how customer behavior and market positioning were shifting over time.

The key modeling challenge was not simply predicting which accounts were at risk of churning — it was identifying where the company's service offerings were drifting out of alignment with a customer's evolving market position. This enabled sales to intervene proactively with the right offer, not just a retention call. Model outputs surfaced directly in the analytics dashboards alongside live campaign and pipeline metrics, giving account managers a single workspace where engagement data and predictive intelligence were co-located.

The Results

"Marketing and sales stopped arguing about whose numbers were right. One version of the truth — and for the first time they could have a conversation about the work instead of the data."

Eliminated weekly manual data collection — teams redirected that time to higher-value strategic and creative work

Five disconnected platforms unified into a single governed data layer with automated, reliable pipelines

Marketing and sales gained a shared real-time dashboard — the first time both functions had visibility into the same numbers simultaneously

Campaign attribution became possible for the first time, enabling data-driven decisions on channel spend and outreach strategy

Institutional data knowledge formalized from individual habits into documented enterprise standards, reducing key-person dependency

Predictive churn and fit scoring models deployed into production dashboards, shifting account management from reactive to proactive

Reporting shifted from backward-looking manual exports to forward-looking, automated, and continuously updated — giving leadership a live pulse on pipeline and customer health
